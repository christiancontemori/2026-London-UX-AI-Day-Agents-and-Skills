# Activity Guidance

## What this day is about

This is a hands-on building day. The goal is not to ship production-ready software. The goal is to experience what it feels like to build something with AI, collaboratively, in real time — and to start forming your own opinion about where it's useful, where it falls short, and what good looks like.

There is no wrong output. There is only starting too late.

---

## Before you begin

Make sure **Code execution and file creation** is enabled in Claude. This unlocks the ability to build interactive tools, run code, and create files directly in the conversation.

- Open Claude desktop
- Click your name in the lower left corner
- Go to **Settings > Capabilities**
- Toggle **Code execution and file creation** on

## How the activity works

### 1. Pick an idea seed and an object

Look at the idea seeds and objects sheet. Pick one seed and combine it with one object. The combination is your starting point, not your brief. Don't spend more than five minutes on this.

The more unexpected the combination, the more interesting the conversation tends to be.

### 2. Start building straight away

Open Claude, describe what you want to build, and see what happens. Don't wait until you have a perfect plan. The act of building is how the plan emerges.

The output can be anything — a working prototype, a research spec, a design concept, a set of prompts, a small tool, a game, a visual. There is no required format. What matters is that you made something, learned something from making it, and can talk about both.

If something doesn't work or doesn't feel right, ask Claude to change it. That is the process.

### 3. Split roles within the team

While some team members are building and iterating, others should be thinking about evaluation. Both roles are equally important.

**Builders** work with Claude to develop the artifact. Their job is to keep momentum — push the idea forward, add features, try things, break things, fix things.

**Evaluators** step back and ask harder questions:

- Does this actually do what we said it would do?
- Where does it fall down?
- Is AI doing the interesting work here, or just the finishing?
- Where did we have to intervene and correct it?
- What would break if you removed AI from this entirely?
- If the artifact uses AI as a core feature — how good are the AI outputs? What makes a response good or bad?

Roles don't have to be fixed. Switch if you want to. But at any given moment someone in the team should be in evaluator mode.

### 4. Iterate as a team

Building with AI is a conversation, not a hand-off. The whole team can contribute ideas, prompts, and directions — you don't need to be the one typing to be meaningfully involved.

When you add a new feature or change direction, pause briefly and ask: does this make the thing better, or are we just adding for the sake of it?

### 5. Share your work

When you have something worth showing, share it with the room in whatever form it exists — a link, a file, a screenshot, a live demo, or just talking through it. There is no single required format for sharing.

## What you can build

The output is whatever is most useful or interesting to your team. Some options:

- A working interactive tool or calculator
- A decision-making aid or game
- A research spec or evaluation framework
- A design concept or set of annotated wireframes
- A set of prompts designed for a specific task
- A small piece of code that does something useful
- A prototype of an interface, experience, or flow
- A written artefact — a brief, a script, a report — generated and refined with AI

Claude Code also lets you build locally with full file system access if you want to go further. This is opt-in and not expected. Ask a facilitator if you want to explore it.

---

## What good looks like

You don't need to finish anything. Good looks like:

- You started something within the first ten minutes
- You iterated at least three or four times
- Someone in the team was thinking about evaluation, not just building
- You have a point of view on at least one thing: what surprised you, what disappointed you, or what you'd do differently
- You can show something to the room, even if it's unfinished

The most interesting debrief conversations tend to come from teams who got stuck, changed direction, and can explain why.

---

## A note on psychological safety

Not everyone in the room has the same level of confidence with code or with AI tools. That is expected and fine. Researchers, designers, and managers all have high-value roles in this activity that have nothing to do with being able to type a prompt fluently.

If you feel lost, there are two places to turn. You can ask a facilitator directly. Or you can use the agent that has been created specifically for today — it knows the context of the day, can help you think about what to build, and can answer questions about how to build with Claude. You'll find instructions for accessing it on the table.

The only thing we'd ask you not to do is wait.
